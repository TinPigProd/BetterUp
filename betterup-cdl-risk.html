Risk and operational management

Holding many moving parts without losing the thread.

A RAID log, Risks, Assumptions, Issues, Dependencies, grounded in a real implementation, and then carried forward into how I'd apply the same discipline to this role.

Context

An enterprise SAP implementation, inventory systems.

At Pure Storage, SAP was brought in to manage inventory systems. Throughout the implementation, it was not clear what training was actually needed. The vendor's instinct was to train against technical documentation. I advocated for training built around the people impacted by the change, what they would need to do differently, not just what the system could technically do.

What happened

The vendor's default approach was to point to technical system documentation as the basis for training. That documentation explained how SAP functioned, not what behavior change was required of the people who would use it differently once it went live. I had to advocate directly for reframing the question: not "what does the system do," but "what does this person need to do differently tomorrow."

What it took

Curiosity, collaboration, and context. It required sitting with the technical services team specifically to understand how SAP inventory data actually needed to flow into their existing ServiceNow workflow, so that when a customer needed a replacement part, the rep could see real-time inventory instead of a manually updated spreadsheet that was perpetually out of date.

Assumption
Multiple administrative layers assumed to need formal training▼
Medium
Learning & Enablement
What happened

Several layers of administrative staff were originally flagged as needing formal training on the new system. Once I spent time in their actual weekly working meetings, it became clear they were already getting the information they needed through that existing rhythm. The assumption that more formal training equals more readiness turned out to be false for this group specifically.

Why this matters

Knowing when not to build something is its own discipline. Resources that would have gone into unnecessary formal training were redirected to where the real gap actually was.

Dependency
Downstream impact to Professional Services and Customer Success surfaced late▼
High
Cross-functional
What happened

While building training specifically for the technical services group, it became clear, late in that process, that Professional Services and Customer Success had their own implications from the same system change that hadn't been scoped or surfaced earlier. The work for one group revealed a dependency that should have been mapped from the start.

The lesson

Solving deeply for the team directly in front of you can obscure who else touches the same workflow. Mapping cross-functional impact needs to happen before deep design work begins, not be discovered as a byproduct of it.

Risk
Training deliverables not ready against an aggressive timeline▼
High
Learning & Enablement
What happened

As the scope ambiguity and cross-functional gaps became clear, I flagged directly that training deliverables were not ready to support the planned go-live timeline. This was not a popular thing to raise on a program already under schedule pressure.

What it cost, honestly

The program rolled twice based on these risks, from an original twelve month timeline to eighteen months, and ultimately past twenty four months. Across that extended window, personnel turnover happened. People moved in and out of the project, which is its own compounding risk, institutional knowledge becomes harder to hold onto the longer a program stretches. I'm not going to pretend the extended timeline was costless. It wasn't. But shipping training that didn't match real behavioral need would have cost more, quietly, after go-live, rather than visibly, before it.

Application

Carrying the same discipline into a client-facing role.

The Pure Storage engagement was internal, the tension was between vendor documentation and user need. In a client-facing role like Client Delivery Director, the same discipline has to extend outward into the relationship itself, where the risk isn't just scope ambiguity, it's a client trying to go off script with the methodology, or decisions fragmenting across multiple stakeholders who were never aligned with each other in the first place.

The job description names this directly: spotting moments where design drift, over-customization, or fragmented decisions could weaken impact, and intervening early to preserve coherence. The RAID items below are how I'd apply the same instinct I used at Pure Storage, advocating for real need over surface-level requests, to that specific risk.

What it looks like

An operating lead asks to skip the Rumbling with Vulnerability module because it feels too uncomfortable for their culture, or a facilitator quietly softens the language session to session without anyone explicitly deciding that was acceptable. Each individual request sounds reasonable. The risk is what accumulates.

How the Pure Storage lesson applies

The instinct that served me well there, asking what the person actually needs rather than accepting the surface request, applies directly here. The response isn't a flat no. It's understanding what the client is actually trying to solve, then being honest about what can flex and what cannot.

Issue
Fragmented decisions across client stakeholders who were never aligned▼
High
Client Delivery Director
What it looks like

Multiple stakeholders on the client side each make small, well-intentioned adjustments independently, an HR lead changes session cadence, an executive sponsor changes which leaders are included, none of which looks like drift in isolation, but which compound into something that no longer resembles what was designed and sold.

How the Pure Storage lesson applies

The Professional Services and Customer Success surprise at Pure Storage came from solving deeply for one group without mapping who else touched the workflow. The same mapping discipline applies to client stakeholders, understanding the full set of people who can influence the program before drift has a chance to compound silently.

Assumption

Medium
Vice President
What it looks like

The sponsor who championed the program at signature may not be the same person present at the executive business review months later, through promotion, reassignment, or departure. Assuming continuity without verifying it is exactly the kind of assumption that needs active tracking, not passive hope.

How the Pure Storage lesson applies

Personnel turnover during the Pure Storage engagement compounded an already difficult timeline. The lesson wasn't that turnover is avoidable, it isn't, it was that tracking it actively rather than being surprised by it changes how much damage it does.
